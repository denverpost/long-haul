How I’d walk the trail next time

Published Nov. 5, 2015

By Dean Krakel

Conifer —

Coming off the trail is strange. I still sleep under my sleeping bag, with the window wide open. I drink water from my trail bottle. My daily attire is the same running shoes, shorts and shirt that I wore on the trail. (If formality’s called for, I’ll put on pants.)

The South Platte River at the Gudy Gaskill Bridge. The bridge divides segments 1 and 2 of The Colorado Trail, and for me, it was the end of my journey — I crossed the bridge and took the North Fork Trail north to my home in Conifer. Dean Krakel, Special to The Denver Post

A fellow hiker asked me if I was still peeing outside at night.

Um … yeah. I am.

A few weeks after leaving The Colorado Trail, I think about it every day. I long to be out there. Walking. Unpacking the pack and putting gear away has been a form of closure. It’s also given me an opportunity to think about what I’ll do different next time.

Food

I won’t attempt to make all my own backpacking food again. All winter long, the Krakel Ranch bustled with preparations for the trail. By spring, I’d produced 24 pounds of pemmican — that light-weight, calorie-dense combination of meat and fat that I was going to rock the trail with — along with homemade energy bars, pinole cookies and dehydrated berries, beans and noodles.

The reality is, on the trail I ate very little of the food I manufactured.

Pemmican dropped into the cook pot and mixed with freeze-dried meals created a nutritious calorie bomb. But inside my pack, pemmican melted into a greasy, smelly mush in the summer heat. Plus, I began thinking of it as a bear attractant, which was a disturbing thought. But the main thing was, after eating several pounds of pemmican, I wasn’t in love with the taste.

My taste in other trail foods changed dramatically, too. I quickly tired of the homemade energy bars I’d packed in my resupply boxes and ended up buying KIND and Pro Bars whenever I was in towns. At home I created meals of rice, chicken, beans and eggs. But on the trail I favored stroganoff, macaroni and pasta. (I will never eat another freeze-dried egg meal.)

I started buying other things in town: Epic and Tanka bison bars, rather than processed jerky. Tuna and salmon, in the foil packets. And cheese, which is heavy, but I never tired of it.

Cooking oatmeal for breakfast quickly got old. I preferred Justin’s almond butter and a Vega protein shake instead. I did use the instant oatmeal I packed, as a snack, pouring it directly into my mouth as I walked. You can’t get much more instant than that.

Amazingly, I didn’t miss coffee on the trail, and I didn’t take candy … but I was always happy when someone packing caffeine and Snickers bars joined me.

Whatever calorie deficit I had on the trail I made up for when I was in town. “That humongous cheeseburger comes with a choice of three sides, which would you like?” “All three and another Moose Drool, please.”

Since I ended up not eating most of the food I mailed to myself, next time around I won’t use resupply boxes. I’ll hitch into town and shop at grocery stores.

Training

Last November, I began seriously training for The Colorado Trail. I hired personal trainer Shawn Austin of Body By Emilee to whip me into shape. Austin, who is a rock climber, distance runner and former Outward Bound instructor, created workouts that elevated my heart rate and created strength, stability and balance, which is especially important for ankles. I did a lot of stair sprints. Instead of running on the local trails as usual, I walked them carrying a heavily weighted backpack.

Training for the trail not only strengthened my body and made it more injury resistant, it also strengthened my mind. The discipline required for training pays off when you crawl out of the tent and begin climbing yet another saddle, yet another ridge. If you’ve been humping a sandbag over hill and dale in rain, snow, darkness and heat, walking up a mountain is just like another training day. Just as important was the confidence I had in my leg and lung power to get the job done.

I attribute Austin’s good training to the fact that I never suffered during my trip, never felt wiped out at day’s end, never had any aches, pains or soreness — until I took that fall while running on Seale Pass. Chances are, without good conditioning I would have gotten more banged up.

Solo hiking

For me the hardest thing about walking The Colorado Trail was going solo. Being alone has its advantages, and I enjoyed it. Sometimes, though, I had to make decisions or walk in places and through situations that I dearly wished I could talk to someone else about. And there’s the whole sharing the experience thing as well. At times it would have been nice to have had company.

But for the most part, The Colorado Trail isn’t a lonely place. There are other hikers. Towns are close. Navigation is easy. But still, when it came right down to it, I was alone much of the time, and being alone, making a mistake, even a simple one, can result in serious complications. As one hiker said to me, “When you’re alone, things can get real, real fast.” Hiking solo I was more cautious, methodical and thoughtful about consequences.

Pines cast shadows along Lost Creek on The Colorado Trail in segment 4. Dean Krakel, Special to The Denver Post

The true secret to hiking alone is comfort. Being comfortable with your skill level and the environment you’re in, comfortable with who you are and what your limitations are. But the most important thing is, you’ve got to be comfortable being by yourself, being your own best company and counsel.

Gear

One of my goals was to travel The Colorado Trail as lightly as possible. I did pretty well with that.

A Zpacks Arc Blast 52L backpack, 23 ounces, carried everything. I liked the Blast’s simplicity and minimalist, low-profile design; it handled loads under 30 pounds well. I had two pouches sewn on each side of the waist belt in which I carried my camera, spare batteries, SD cards, reporter’s notebook (with the cardboard covers removed) and a pen.

And that’s what I carried. Total weight, with five days of food, but no water, was 22 pounds, 4 ounces.

Other gear

I used an Olympus TG-4 point and shoot camera. Water, dust, freeze and drop proof, and evidently Dean proof, I carried this camera in my shirt pocket for 11 weeks and was always amazed by the quality of images it was capable of producing. Battery life was excellent. One battery lasted a week or longer; I carried four extras. The TG-4 also had the ability to create a wifi hotspot anywhere, allowing me to transfer images from the camera to my iPhone for editing and sending back to the blog.

I wore Saucony Peregrine 5 trail running shoes, a size larger than what I normally wear. Peregrines have incredible traction on every type of terrain. No complaints, even minor, about them. I wore out three pair. Inside the shoes, green Superfeet insoles.

In addition to the Zpacks tarp, I used two other shelters during the trip: a Big Agnes Fly Creek UL2 tent, 2 pounds, and a Big Agnes Scout UL2 tarp tent, 2 pounds. I weathered storms in the San Juans in the Fly Creek; it was the tent I most often saw other hikers carrying. The Scout, a combination of both tent and tarp, was easy to set up and turned storms and combined all the best features of both worlds. It was well-vented, and I liked having a tarp with a floor and a door.

During the first half of my trip, I used an Enlightened Equipment Revelation down quilt, 20 ounces. The quilt took some getting used to; I slept in my down sweater and long underwear to aid in additional coverage and warmth. I never slept cold … until that frigid morning at Tank Seven Creek, when I decided I needed a warmer bag.

Magic miles

Published Oct. 23, 2015

By Dean Krakel

When October came and I was still on The Colorado Trail, it occurred to me that I might not finish.

Chair Rocks rises above the Buffalo Creek burn area. The fire in 1996 ran 11 miles in four and a half hours and burned 11,900 acres. Dean Krakel, Special to The Denver Post

Winter had been nipping at my heels all the way from the Tenmile Range. Although I had hiked most of the trail without pains or aches, now a blister was hatching on my right foot, and I felt tweaks in my left achilles and knee that worried me. If the shoulder and rib injuries from my fall on Searle Pass worsened, well, that could put an end to things, too. I wasn’t the big dog for pain that I thought I was.

When I’d topped out on Georgia Pass on Oct. 5, I’d felt a happiness that moved me to tears. Georgia was a familiar place, and the last big pass standing between me and my home in Conifer. The wind was blowing hard and the tears were mingling with snowflakes and sleet on my face. I couldn’t tell which was which. The trail had stripped me down, brought emotions close to the surface, left my soul as transparent as a mica flake.

The next morning in my camp near Deadman Creek on the west side of Kenosha Pass I awoke in so much pain I was forced to make a tough decision. I couldn’t go on the way things were. I needed to leave the trail and spend some time healing. But if I left, would I really be able to come back. What if the comforts of home proved too seductive or winter arrived with a vengeance in my absence.

But a week later I was back on the trail with a smile on my face, going down the east side of Kenosha Pass. The last 70 miles of The Colorado Trail was in front of me. It was warm, warm enough for shorts! For the first time since falling, I could hold a hiking pole in my left hand and take deep breaths without wincing. I was carrying a light pack over easy, rolling terrain, kicking up dry leaves with every step, gazing skyward to a golden aspen canopy and cloudless blue.

I was going to make it.

Kenosha Pass, the Lost Creek Wilderness, the Buffalo Creek burn — I’d imagined that these final sections of trail would be interesting. But they were more than that. They were some of the most beautiful, lonely and joyful passages on my journey. Magical miles.

In the Lost Creek Wilderness, the summits of the Kenosha Mountains are knobby, brooding summits reaching up to 12,000 feet and extending for more than 30 miles. The meadows are piled with granite boulders and domes and filled with aspen and pine groves. Lost Creek itself pours down through a wide, pine-framed meadow afire with the reds and yellows of willows and tall grasses in early October, and the muddy banks of beaver ponds are tracked up by deer, elk and small critters of all kinds. And there was more mountain lion scat in the trail through the Lost Creek Wilderness than I’d ever seen in my life, which added to the wildness.

Every now and then I’d look back and see blue silhouettes of mountains on the horizon and think about how far I’d come, from beyond those mountains and the mountains beyond them, from the far southwest corner of the state.

It was too much to comprehend. That high, raw alpine world of steep-sided peaks, summer wildflowers, snowfields, expanses of tundra and streams falling in torrents seemed like a distant planet I’d visited ages ago.

Aspens were in all stages of their autumn foliage change along segment 4 of The Colorado Trail in mid-October. Dean Krakel, Special to The Denver Post

Sometimes as I walked I’d try and remember all of my camps. I could remember the rain camp on the first night out of Canyon Creek, the big storm camp beneath Indian Trail Ridge, my bear camp near Hotel Draw, lightning camp at Hancock Lake, the full moon at Camp Hale. But in two and a half months on the trail, most had become a blur. There were so many blanks in my memory, I gave up playing that game.

I thought I’d be able to figure my life out on the trail. The past. The future. What happens next. Somewhere along the way, I thought there would be a moment of satori, what Buddhists call a realization of one’s true nature. A pine tree would burst into flame and a thunderous voice would boom, “Hey Deano, here’s the big plan.” Never happened. By the time I reached Lost Creek, I’d pretty much thought about everything there was to think about until I couldn’t think about those things anymore and I had come to the realization that there was nothing I could do about any of it, anyway.

With all the mental and physical fat burned away — I’d lost nearly 15 pounds — and not enough food left to be distracted by snacking, I became a guy just walking the trail. Walking guy decided to spend the remainder of the journey being mindful and present. That was my satori, not a flaming pine tree and booming voice. A light pack and an enlightened mind. Something like that.

Time and miles flowed by. I was astounded to find myself walking a 3 mile-per-hour pace. Yet there was still time to take photographs, linger by springs and creeks, study animal tracks and scat, listen to the squirrels and birds, goof around and nap.

I rolled right through the Rolling Creek Trailhead into segment 3 and camped among the Ponderosa pines, on a soft bed of pine needles and kinnickinnic. It’s interesting, camping in familiar country I’d only seen during the daytime as a mountain biker or running man. Now I was drinking from tiny streams I’d splashed thoughtlessly through before and seeing things I’d never really paid attention to.

Views from my last camp, just below the Buffalo Creek burn area, above the South Platte River, a country of rocky knobs and domes. Dean Krakel, Special to The Denver Post

A friend from Conifer hiked in to meet me at the tail end of that stretch, and as we walked we talked about life changes over the past few months. She helped me locate a beer another friend had stashed for me (“go past the gate with the ‘Closed’ sign, find a tree with a pink ribbon around it, then look beside the fallen log nearby”) and drove me to the firehouse to get water from the spigot in back.

People have asked me whether after being alone for so long I minded having company on the trail. Friends and family joining me for a segment or a few miles wasn’t an intrusion. It was support and love. This Colorado Trail trip wasn’t just about miles and scenery. Whatever experience the trail provided had become the journey.

Before light the next morning, I entered the Buffalo Creek burn and watched the sun come up over that surreal landscape, miles and miles of burned trees standing ghostlike in the pale light. A controlled fire to the southeast provided a smoky mist, adding to the haunted effect.

I feel a deep connection with the burn. I’d been a photographer at the Rocky Mountain News when the fires and floods raged in 1996. Later, my wife and our boys often mountain biked on The Colorado Trail there, and when I’d visited the burn in my other life, before walking The Colorado Trail, all I saw was a devastation, a post-fire endpoint that saddened me.

Walking in the big mountains I had felt small and insignificant. The geologic scale and the cataclysmic events that had shaped things was staggering. But it was also in the distant past. Hiking through the burn was like walking through an open wound. I felt close to the raw, brute force of nature and the striving desire of living things to burst forth. Far from a dead zone, the burn was full of life, regrowth and color.

By leaving my job and all the precious things in my life behind to walk The Colorado Trail, I had, in a way, created my own personal forest fire. The act of walking sometimes felt like an internal burn. I’d like to think that a new me will rise out of the ashes, but like the Buffalo Creek burn, it’s a process that’s still evolving. And it’ll take some time to see just exactly what comes forth.

That night, I camped under the stars on a grassy hump below the burn. In the morning after an eleven week, 500-plus mile hike through the Colorado mountains, I’d walk down the switchbacks to the South Platte River, cross the Gudy Gaskill bridge, turn up the North Fork Trail and walk home. But as I laid awake staring into the night sky, I didn’t think of the ending of things. I thought about how grateful I was to be lying beneath the pines and stars one more time, listening to the river with the big, wild world stretching out around me. And in that moment, that was all I needed.

Dean Krakel pauses at home after finishing his thru-hike of The Colorado Trail. Dean Krakel, Special to The Denver Post

Dean Krakel completed his thru-hike of The Colorado Trail on the evening of Oct. 15, 2015.

Dean has been hiking the trail from south to north. The perfect hitch, and the best resupply stops

Published Oct. 14, 2015

By Dean Krakel

Frisco —

For thru-hikers, getting to town is a huge thing. Town is where the coffee, cheeseburger and shower gods live. Getting from town back to the trail is another huge thing. And hitching is how I (and most hikers) get back and forth to resupply and then get back on the trail.

While hiking The Colorado Trail this season, I’ve hitched to and from: Silverton, Durango, Lake City, Gunnison, Salida, Buena Vista, Crested Butte, Frisco, Breckenridge, Mount Princeton Hot Springs, Leadville, Cottonwood Pass, Monarch Pass, Colorado 114, Durango, Hotel Draw, Molas Pass. Some of them, many times.

Resupplying on backpacking trips is new to me. In the past, if I backpacked for two weeks, I carried two weeks supply of food on my back. But there’s no point in carrying pounds and pounds of food when I can cut weight by restocking from a grocery store or picking up a general delivery package at the post office.

The downside: hitching to town requires time and patience. You have to wait along the road, usually out in the middle of nowhere, for a ride.

Krakel’s thumb’s out for a ride outside of Buena Vista. Dean Krakel, Special to The Denver Post

Conversations about hitching among hikers mainly revolve around how hard or easy the hitch is. But I think it’s a matter of serendipity and karma. Hitchers and hitchees have something to give each other, and usually it’s more than just a ride.

Strangers can be totally candid with one another. A woman who picked me up in Gunnison was trying to decide whether to quit her job or not and pursue some life-long dreams. Having just left my job to walk The Colorado Trail I probably wasn’t the best person for her to talk to. Or maybe I was.

Lake City was the hardest hitch I’ve had. Many drivers slowed down enough to tell me they were only going a short ways, otherwise they’d give me a lift. It took nearly an hour standing on the edge of town before a guy servicing backcountry yurts picked me up.

Gunnison was the second hardest. I spent a long, hot morning attempting to hitch out of town. Finally, a guy pulled over, told me I was standing in the wrong place and took me to the right place — in front of the Walmart, where I was picked up within minutes and delivered to Crested Butte.

The easiest hitches were Buena Vista and Salida. Stand anywhere along U.S. 285 or 50 with your pack and someone is going to pick you up.

The absolute best hitch I’ve had so far, in fact the most nearly perfect hitch I’ve had, went like this.

My friend Tom had come out to hike the final 40 miles of the Collegiate West trail with me. We’d left his car on Cottonwood Pass and now we were walking the final miles along Monarch Ridge to U.S. 50. The sky had been threatening rain all day. We’d just popped out onto the highway and had no more than stuck our thumbs out when a fireman from Gunnison whipped off the road and popped open his trunk. We’d just gotten into his car when a pouring rain started.

The fireman took us to the parking area in front of the Monarch Crest store on top of Monarch Pass. The rain stopped. Five minutes later, a woman who’d been hiking the nearby Crest Trail picked us up. She had been unemployed for a long time but had recently gone back to school and reinvented herself as a software designer. She drove us all the way to the post office in Salida, where I had a resupply box waiting.

Picking up my box at the post office, one of the people waiting in line struck up a conversation. He and his girlfriend, both ultrarunners, wanted to run The Colorado Trail. He bought us coffee, then took us out of town to U.S. 285 to hitch a ride to Buena Vista.

Our coffee was still steaming as a traveling hospice nurse stopped and waved us into her car. It’d started to rain by then, and we did look pretty pathetic standing there with our packs, my cardboard resupply box rapidly getting soaked.

As she drove us to Buena Vista, I asked her what the most important thing was that she’d learned from her patients.

“Forgiveness,” she said. “Forgiveness, and that the dying thought the living were wasting precious time.”

The nurse took us as far as her house, which just happened to be on the road going to Cottonwood Pass. We’d no more than unloaded from her car when a red pickup pulled over.

“I can take you as far as the hot springs,” the driver said. “Jump in back.” And so we did.

One of the most important things I’ve learned about hitching: No distance is too short. Any forward momentum is good.

At Cottonwood Hot Springs, we waited just a few minutes. An elk hunting guide on his way over the pass stopped and welcomed us to hop into his SUV. He was late to his camp — dinner was waiting! — and he drove fast up the pass while we talked about elk, and their amazing vision, and the prehistoric hunting pits Tom and I had seen that morning on the Continental Divide.

All of this, and in just under two hours, we’d been deposited at Tom’s car on the top of Cottonwood Pass.

That’s what I call a perfect hitch.

Dean Krakel’s pack and feet in the back of a pickup on the way to Cottonwood Pass, the final segment of the perfect hitch back to the trail. Dean Krakel, Special to The Denver Post

Ghost’s best of hitching towns:

Cheeseburger: The Packer burger at the Packer Saloon and Cannibal Grill, Lake City. The Gunnisack in Gunnison serves up a pretty tasty burger as well.

Beer and a bite: Eddyline Brewery and Restaurant in Buena Vista. They serve their own brews here and the food is delicious. Giant salads. You don’t get those on the trail.

Motel: The Prospector Motel in Silverton. Real coffee in the rooms, delivery to the trailhead when you’re ready and they take resupply boxes. And the price is right: $40 bucks a night.

Grocery store: The High Country Market in Lake City. Big selection of hiker foods, fresh produce, a great deli and other supplies. They even have a sign on the door: Hiker Trash Welcome Here. Pete, one of the owners, takes hiker boxes and goes out of his way to be helpful.

Library: Silverton. Located in a cool old historic building with a chill, dog-friendly atmosphere and Mac computers. Runner-up: The library in Frisco has a huge selection of used paperback books for a buck. My worst library experience, by the way, was in Buena Vista. They have an hour limit, per person, per day and no exceptions, even if there’s a fleet of computers not being used.

Best outdoor shop: The Trailhead in Buena Vista. They have everything, well most everything, a hiker could need in the way of supplies, and the staff is very helpful. Salida Mountain Sports in Salida is also good, but it has more clothing than backpacking supplies.

Best coffee shops: The Roastery in Buena Vista. A laid-back place with good food. It’s OK to linger here; I spent hours writing at The Roastery. The Mocha Moose in Lake City is also excellent, with a great burrito and homemade salsa.

Best breakfast burrito: The Butterhorn Bakery and Cafe in Frisco. Huge breakfast burrito, endless coffee refills.

Best public transportation: Summit County’s free buses run all the time and go everywhere: service to Frisco, Leadville, Breckenridge, Silverthorne and Dillon.

Best post office: Salida. Friendly, helpful staff who go out of their way to help hikers with problem shipments.

Fear on the trail, real and imagined

By Dean Krakel

Conifer —

Until a few nights ago, I hadn’t thought about mountain lions much as I walked The Colorado Trail.

Lions I’ve not seen or heard. Two hikers I talked to saw mountain lions, and both times it was in the Cochetopa Hills, and both times the cat was lazily stretched out across the trail and took off the minute it became aware of humans.

I’m not a fan of the big cats. I’ve seen one mountain lion in my lifetime, and that was in my Conifer front yard. It was quick, lithe and beautiful. But that’s plenty. My life list of mammals in that regard is complete.

The Tenmile Range, from the high country between Kokomo and Searle passes on segment 8 of The Colorado Trail. Dean Krakel, Special to The Denver Post

One dark and stormy night last week, I was climbing up a ridge through deadfall and thick trees, looking for a place to camp. There hadn’t been a flat space big enough to pitch the tarp for miles, not even a flat enough spot to stretch out in. Lightning was flashing in the distance and thunder was rumbling overhead. Every now and then the sky spit down frozen sleet. If there was nothing down low, I thought, there might be a flat spot up on top, hopefully sheltered by trees.

It was growing dark, so I put my headlamp on and kept moving. The problem was, I wasn’t moving very well. I’d taken that tumble up on Searle Pass a few days before, and everything hurt.

Although the rotator cuff in my left shoulder was the big concern, my whole left arm was pretty much useless, and my right shoulder had started acting up as well. Getting my pack on and off had become an ordeal involving tree stumps or boulders for props, rolling around on the ground and of course, cursing. My ribs ached every time I took a deep breath. My left knee was tweaked in the fall, too. I had to stop and rest often, more often than usual, to hunch over my one hiking pole and take light, non-hurtful breaths.

Finally topping out on the ridge, I saw a nice looking campsite, but you know how it is: Hey, there may be something better around the next bend. So I sauntered on down the trail. Then I stopped cold.

I had the weirdest sensation that I was being shadowed. My senses were tingling and the hair on the back of my neck was prickling. There was fresh bear scat full of berries in the trail at my feet, but that didn’t bother me at all. Bears are curious but black bears aren’t stalkers.

No. Not bear. Something else. That was when my mind screamed, lion! Nothing attracts the attention of a predator like a weak or injured animal. And it was late evening, dinner time. I did a rapid fire review of everything I knew about a lion confrontation: I couldn’t raise my arms overhead to make myself appear bigger. I couldn’t easily drop my pack and yank out the umbrella, my strongest defense. My Swiss Army Knife was buried somewhere inside the pack. Running wouldn’t be an option.

I stopped. There was no way I was going around the next trail bend. Even if I’d wanted too my feet wouldn’t have moved me there. Every fiber of my being was humming on high alert. Caveman stuff.

Now, the odds of something happening between man and beast on The Colorado Trail are very low, almost nonexistent. To my knowledge, no one has ever been killed or injured or even greatly hassled by any large critter in the whole history of the trail. Nor has anyone been killed by lightning on the trail, and lightning is a real and major danger. Falling trees haven’t harmed anyone.

Moose, for me, are the next most worrisome thing: Surprising a moose can be a really unfortunate experience. Bears are around but, like I said, in two and a half months I’ve seen one track and mainly, bears just want you to go away. People ask me if I’ve seen any bears and I always have to say no, but I’ll bet lots of bears have seen me. Certainly they’ve smelled me. And that’s got to be scary for them.

But mountain lions have killed people in Colorado and elsewhere. They’re killing machines, and they speak to something deep down and dark in our very core.

Max Young digs in his pack for rain gear with a rainbow in the background and the Tenmile Range in the distance. Dean Krakel, Special to The Denver Post

I don’t want to be culled from the herd, don’t want to be the first person on The Colorado Trail to go down in that particular record book. I suspect I already hold the longest thru-hiking record. No addendums please. “Oh, old Ghost. Yeah, he was slow. And he got mauled by a lion, too. Perhaps he should have been faster. Tisk, tisk, tisk.”

As quietly as possible, I began walking backwards. When I got to a wide spot in the trail, I whipped around and made my way back to that really great and now very appealing camping spot I’d seen. I removed my pack as gently as possible while standing fully upright, no wincing or cussing, then opened it, dug out the tarp and set it up one-handed with much louder banging of rocks against the stakes than usual.

See here, lion! Man, rock, metal, tool! Then I crawled inside, zippered it up and went to bed without supper.

I didn’t see a lion, but then, mostly, you never do.

I’m asked often if I get scared walking alone on The Colorado Trail. I have to say, that was the scariest experience I’ve had. And I never saw anything tangible to fear. For the most part I don’t think about everything that can go wrong on the trail, because lots can go wrong and we can’t do anything about the majority of it. If you think about everything that can go wrong, and what there is to be afraid of, you won’t ever take a step.

The most dangerous thing in the woods are humans, and the most dangerous things that happen to humans on The Colorado Trail, we do to ourselves. Like running up a pass in the near dark wearing a big pack.

I have bumped into one section hiker who was packing a huge handgun in a shoulder holster. “Why?” I asked. “For bears and cats and such,” he said.

Packing a gun on the trail isn’t the answer for me. A gun doesn’t change the fear factor enough to make up for the weight, and when I’m in the woods with a gun, I have an altered attitude. It’s not the mindset I want to be in on the trail. I’m out there as a fellow traveler, a visitor, an observer. I’m out there as a humble pacifist, alert, watchful and respectful.

But I do carry a big umbrella.

I couldn’t shoot my camera fast enough. An amazing landscape would unfold, I’d take a picture or many and five minutes later that vision would be replaced by something even more wonderful.

We’d been moving steadily all day. As the daylight faded, we were busting hard for Searle Pass when the last five minutes of daylight cast a red glow on the highest peaks all around. I began to run.

I opened and closed the fist of my left arm and nearly passed out.

Truthfully, I don’t think much about the future. There’s too much in the present to be concerned with. Like the weather. Winter’s coming. Take my mind off the trail and think about something and the next thing I know I’m stumbling over a root or a rock.

And I’ve finally figured out the whole squirrel thing … but that’s another story.

The longer I’ve been on the trail, the less important it has become to be hasty and the more important it has become to see and savor. I’ll pass this way just once, so I’m in no hurry. I’ve no job waiting for me. No partner. No wife. No girlfriend. My boys are grown.

I’ve still got 117 miles to go until I turn the door knob on the front door of my home in Conifer. A two-week, 100-mile backpacking trip is still a pretty formidable adventure, and, joyfully, that’s ahead of me.

Mainly, I feel blessed to still be out here on the trail. Taking that first step at Canyon Creek in Durango was scary. There was so much ahead of me and so much I didn’t know. I’m a changed man now.

I feel competent and comfortable in the woods in a way I’ve never felt before. How that will translate in the “real” world in front of me I don’t know.

What I do know is this: I’m moving on. Snow is coming and I’ve got two high passes to get over. This morning I dressed myself in under 30 minutes. I’m getting some limited range of motion in the arm. With a light pack, a pocket full of pain pills and the blessings of the universe, I’m good to go for the distance ahead.

And as my friend Winston Caine reminded me: Having one arm never stopped The Fugitive.

There’s no one to say, “Hey, look at that!” No one to discuss dinner decisions with. No one to make weather speculations with or to puzzle over strange forks in the trail with. There’s no one to share in the misery of wet socks and wet shoes and a cold morning start. You get the picture.

There used to be other hikers on the trail to socialize with and catch up on the news with. Not now. There’s no one to ask about anything and no one to blame for anything. Just me. I’m the only one out here.

Being alone it’s hard to self-motivate, to get up and get going. Truthfully, some mornings I just want to lay wrapped in the warmth of the sleeping bag.

Keep moving. That’s my mantra, rain or shine, long or short, slow or fast. Keep moving. Except when it’s time to nap.

Not everyone that sets out from Denver to Durango or vice versa finishes the trail. When I land in a town, I hear news about people I met along the way leaving the trail. There’s all kinds of obstacles on the trail, and not all of them of the wild variety. Blisters, dehydration, medical and family emergencies, cold, heat, burnout, loneliness, sickness. All take their toll on hikers.

I give myself permission to turn back, to hike out to a road and take the big hitch back to Denver every day. And by doing so, I also give myself permission to go on.

Or so I thought.

Crossing the fearsome 30-mile tundra

Published Aug. 14, 2015

By Dean Krakel

Columbines on the 30-mile tundra, on segment 24. Dean Krakel, Special to The Denver Post

Spring Creek Pass, Lake City —

Hikers call it the “30-mile tundra,” and it’s one of the most beautiful and feared sections of The Colorado Trail.

Segments 22-24 stretch along the spine of the San Juan Mountains from Molas Pass to San Luis Pass (for northbound hikers, like me) — a lot of mileage. Fifty miles, if you add it up, but people call it 30. You can’t get any higher or wilder in Colorado than the Continental Divide. You’re rolling along between 12,000 and 13,000 feet for days at a time and up there, in the boiling storm clouds hovering in the peaks. Up there is where the lightning god lives. There is nowhere to run, nowhere to hide. There are no trees. There is no cover. You are the tallest thing in the boulder-pocked green. You are the lightning rod.

All the way up the crazy-steep climb along roaring Elk Creek to its headwaters on the Divide, in the rains and the mists ghosting among the peaks and lush forests of the Weminuche Wilderness, I kept asking other hikers I met about what was ahead, what was it like up there, how did they handle it, and the answer was always the same: something along the lines of, “dude, we got lucky.”

“We ran 20 miles,” said Rafikki, who was hiking with Wildflower and Breeze. “We got a window in the weather and we went for it.”

“What can you do?” said Steamer with a shrug of his shoulders. “You can’t do anything about it. There’s nothing you can do. Find some low depression and wait it out and hope it’s not your time I guess.”

Not comforting. Not comforting at all. My mind was racing as I toiled up the trail under a heavy load — because I’d just resupplied in Silverton, and it had rained all night the night before, and I’d packed the tent and the rain fly wet in the interest of speed.

By the time I finished the final million switchbacks to the Divide I had pretty well inventoried everything that could possibly go wrong. It’s well known that to avoid death by lightning in the Colorado high country, you get up and off the top by noon, because storms usually happen later in the day — usually. OK. But what if you couldn’t get up and down by noon? What if when you got to the top, that’s all there is? And what if you have to be on top for days? And what if you were carrying lots of metal: hiking poles, tent poles, stakes, umbrella, the eyelets in my gaiters, African bracelet, knife. Fillings. Sheesh. I was practically a metal magnet.

So I’d worked myself into a good state by the time I reached the top, at 12,000 feet. Wind-whipped clouds swirled above me and all around. On every horizon were clouds of every description and temperament: wind shredded wisps and mare’s tails, black and grey thunderheads, white cotton puff balls. Storms flickered across distant peaks. I pulled down the hood of my rain shell so I could hear better. What was that? Thunder? No, a high flying jet. Was that a high flying jet? No. It’s thunder. Distant. But still. Thunder is thunder.

And so I began walking. Fast. Always studying the sky. Always looking for a low depression I could squeeze into. A place among the rocks where I wasn’t the highest point in the landscape. A ravine I could sprint down. A cliff I could jump off. Yikes! It was nerve-wracking. I thought about turning back. I don’t have to do this. I thought about the lightning storm I was in last year while I was doing the Collegiate East and West trails, how I threw off my pack and tossed my poles away from me and squatted on my haunches while the storm raged overhead and I, who do not pray, was moved to prayer. “I’ll be such a better person. I’ll be so good. I’ll turn a new leaf. I’ll give money to hospitals and orphanages and the homeless. Just let me through this one please.” And I thought about Pounce and Helicopter, two Continental Divide hikers who’d been caught in the same storm, only much higher, on Hope Pass. By the time they reached their resupply in the tiny town of Twin Lakes, Colorado, Pounce was in tears. “I’ve walked all the way from Mexico. I just want to go to Canada,” she said. “I don’t want to die.”

Looking down the switchbacks of Elk Creek from the Continental Divide at Mile 396. Dean Krakel, Special to The Denver Post

After a time that first day I began to relax. I couldn’t remain in such a high state of vigilance, so aware, all my senses humming at full alert. I started to enjoy the flowers. I stopped to take pictures of the clouds. I studied the rocks. I looked at elk tracks and laughed at the goofy marmots who always seemed to be caught napping along the sides of the trail. The tall rock cairns built as trail markers became a strange sort of company, like rock people, a sign not only showing the way but a sign that others had been here. Many others. A lot of others before me, and so far as I knew, not one of them had died in that high, lonely environment.

That night, with my tent pitched next to a tiny stream trickling out of a snowbank, was the first time in my Colorado Trail journey that I had no cellphone coverage. Before then I’d been able to turn my phone on at night and reach out and touch people to let them know I was alright. Now, for the next eight days I would have no contact. I was truly on my own. Alone. And it felt good.

While I was Silverton, during my resupply I called Laine Walter, blind hiker Trevor Thomas’s friend and supporter. We were chatting away, I was sorting out food and feeling lazy, like maybe I could spend another zero day just hanging out, when suddenly Laine blurted out. “You have to move. You have to take whatever window of opportunity opens and you have to go with it. You have to keep moving. That’s the name of the game in long-distance hiking. Move, Dean!”

I thought about those words over and over again in the 30-mile-tundra. I learned how eat breakfast, doctor my blisters and pack my tent from the inside out. I learned to pack and separate wet gear, and when was a good time to stop and dry all that gear. I learned when was a good time to nap or rest or eat. What clouds to watch. What winds to keep track of. I learned how to be my own best company. But most of all I learned how to move, and how to keep moving no matter what the weather was doing, or how I felt about things.

And that is how I got across the 30-mile-tunda.

By Dean Krakel

The minute you start thinking about thru-hiking the trail, you have to start thinking about resupply. Your resupply determines what kind of food you’re going to make, how durable it is, how long it lasts. Then you have to decide whether to mail resupply boxes or drop them off yourself — that’s what I chose to do, because you can’t mail butane canisters.

Calculating the food you take on the trail and your resupplies has to be done carefully. Are you going to arrive at a resupply empty? That’s my goal. For the longer stretches between towns and resupplies, I hope I’ve figured that right.

Here’s my resupply plan — and remember I’m starting in Durango — with some trail description along the way.

Segments 28-25: This stretch goes from the start at Junction Creek in Durango and travels 73.9 miles to Molas Pass, on U.S. 550. My resupply will be at Little Molas Lake campground, or Silverton. It is a frontier for me — I’ve not seen it in any of my previous Colorado Trail hikes. It climbs into the San Juan Mountains, above treeline. This stretch is high and exposed and I’ll need to move fast to avoid storms. I won’t have my trail legs yet, and pack organization will be sketchy for a few days. This is a country of big sweeping views and the flowers should be outrageously beautiful. I expect this stretch to take five days.

Segments 24-22: From Molas Pass, The Colorado Trail travels 53.3 miles to Spring Creek Pass, through more wide-open country at high elevation. In these segments the trail crosses Coney Summit, which is over 13,000 feet and thus the highest point on the Colorado Trail. My resupply will be in Lake City; I’ll hitch 17 miles on Colorado 149 to get there. I’ve been over this section before, long ago, not long after The Colorado Trail was completed. What I remember most is that there weren’t any other hikers in eight days of traveling. My other big memory is of one of the worst lightning storms I’ve ever been caught in outdoors. Travel time: 3-4 days.

Segments 21-15: Spring Creek Pass to U.S. 50. This is a long section, 105 miles, and the furthest distance between resupply points. There’s simply nowhere to resupply, unless you walk to Creede (which is out of the way), or a friend resupplies by Jeep at Bear Town, a ghost town, or at the Eddiesville trailhead. This section is also one of the most beautiful on the entire Colorado Trail. The above-treeline expanse of Snow Mesa is one of the highlights and one of the most exposed places on the trail. The Colorado Trail also crosses the Durango/Silverton Narrow Gauge Railroad tracks at Elk Park. The trail climbs up past the peaks of the Grenadier Range, paralleling Elk Creek to the divide, plunges down to Bear Town, crosses the Rio Grande and travels through the lowlands of the Cochetopa Hills before climbing up to Marshall Pass and U.S. 50 … where in my case, a friend will be waiting with a resupply box. Otherwise, with some planning, you could hitch a ride on the train to Silverton or Durango.

Segments 14-11: U.S. 50 to Twin Lakes is a 76-mile stretch that I originally had broken into two portions: one resupply at Mt. I’ve combined this resupply into one drop at Twin Lakes. There’s not much in Twin Lakes, but it’s a bright-enough spot for a person that’s been backpacking for awhile, and the general store will hold hiker packages. Breckenridge sits right on The Colorado Trail. Although I’ve been from Twin Lakes to Tennessee Pass, I’ve not been through the country from there to Kokomo and Searle passes, and I’ve not been from Searle Pass to Breckenridge, which involves a high crossing of the Ten Mile Range. The Holy Cross Wilderness is a beautiful highlight on this section, and the trail also passes through the old army training post, Camp Hale, where the 10th Mountain Division honed their skills during World War II.

Segments 6-1: Breckenridge to Denver, 105 miles. The home stretch! I may resupply in Jefferson, on U.S. 285 here, or at Kenosha Pass. Although I’ve only been from Breckenridge to Georgia Pass once, and that was on a mountain bike, I’ve snowshoed and skied and run and biked and hiked all of the trails from Georgia Pass back to Denver, so this is familiar country. After climbing up and over Georgia Pass and then Kenosha Pass, the trail travels through amazing aspen and pine forest, including through the old Hayman fire burn area — a hot, dry, waterless chunk of trail. After crossing the North Fork of the South Platte River on the Gudy Gaskill bridge (hey, I’m almost home), The Colorado Trail makes a short climb and then a short downhill that leads to the flat old road of Waterton Canyon and the northern terminus of the trail. Yahoo!
