The Prep

4. Final Choices

Denver Post photo editor Dean Krakel plans to spend six weeks hiking The Colorado Trail. It’s time to make final decisions before the start of the big hike.

As snow clears off The Colorado Trail, final prep before the hike

By Dean Krakel

All spring, the buzz on The Colorado Trail network — Instagram, Twitter, blog posts, Facebook — was the snow. Arapahoe Basin ski area received 5 feet of the heavy white stuff in May alone. Even in June the storms continued. Hikers were sinking up to their chests in snow and still not touching bottom. Snow made travel and navigation difficult. There were stories of scary avalanche conditions, ice axes, crampons and ropes.

Most of these postholing and snowshoeing hikers were coming up from the south, on the Continental Divide Trail. These hardy backpackers are hiking from the Mexican border 3,000 miles to Canada. Starting in March and April traversing New Mexico, Divide hikers arrive in Colorado smacking up against the San Juan Mountains in spring conditions.

Now, finally, the high passes are passable. The Colorado Trail has been in my thoughts every day for the past year. Now I feel it drawing close. Soon it will be time to begin the journey. Soon I will be out there.

A backpacker on the Collegiate West portion of The Colorado Trail approaches the crest of Lake Ann Pass. Photo by Dean Krakel

In the meantime there are still some decisions to be made.

While I have been waiting around for the snow to melt (and working), I have also had time to refine the gear I will be carrying. I have made a few significant changes. A lighter backpack (17 ounces), bivy bag (6.4) and a rain kilt (1.9 — yup, a kilt), all made by Zpacks. The tent-versus-tarp, stove-versus-eating cold and even the fleece-versus-down jacket debates … the jury is still out on all of that. I’ve added a good camera, an Olympus T-G 4, which is water, freeze, dust and drop proof. We will see whether it’s Dean and Colorado Trail proof. Right now my pack weighs 13.6 pounds with the tent, a Big Agnes Fly Creek UL2, and a large 13-ounce gas cannister for the stove; a smaller stove canister, 4 ounces, and just the tarp make that weight 11.6. (My dehydrator conked out on me, but the scale lives on.)

Twenty-four pounds of pemmican, pinole and yucca root are in the freezer awaiting deployment. Thus far none of my friends have sickened or died after sampling my experimental backpacking food concoctions. After taking some of my pinole energy cookies into the Arctic, my friend Richard Murphy reports that “one left out for 10 days is still tasty even after scraping mold off the exterior. And I am still alive.”

The Colorado Trail Foundation

The Colorado Trail is administered by The Colorado Trail Foundation, which has a two-person staff sequestered in a tiny office in Golden.

Bill Manning is the executive director of The Colorado Trail Foundation, chief fundraiser and trail boss for 500 to 600 volunteers scattered up and down 566 miles of trail. Manning and his office assistant, Lauren, are the voice of The Colorado Trail in letters, e-mails and brochures, over the telephone, in person and on the foundation’s Facebook page.

Nearly every thru-hiker on the trail will at some point, in some way, interact with Manning, who has “hiked the trail, every inch, by foot, horse and bike.”

The entire year’s budget, Manning says, “is somewhere around $300,000,” built through private money, a few grants, but mainly by individual donations. The Colorado Trail is cared for by “one $100 donation at a time,” he says.

Bill Manning in The Colorado Trail Foundation office in Golden. Photo by Dean Krakel

The Colorado Trail was conceived in 1973 by the Roundup Riders of the Rockies, but not connected end to end until 1987.

Although the trail doesn’t have one true founder, it was mainly through the bulldog persistence and inspiration of Gudy Gaskill that it was completed. There’s a bridge on The Colorado Trail across the North Fork of the South Platte River that bears her name.

The trail is 486 miles long from Denver to Durango. The addition last year of the Collegiate West trail makes the grand total 566 miles. It climbs a total of 89,354 feet, crosses eight mountain ranges and travels through six national forests and six wilderness areas.

For 315 miles The Colorado Trail and Continental Divide Trail are one and the same.

Although no official records are kept, the youngest known thru-hiker to complete the trail was 8½ years old and the oldest was 78. The fastest known end-to-end time is 8 days, 7 hours, 40 minutes. In 1988, 13 people thru-hiked The Colorado Trail; in 1989, only three did it. In 2014 about 250 hikers thru-hiked the trail.

Return to story

Meals of freeze-dried and dehydrated pastas, chilis and refried beans are bagged, tagged and ready to be sorted into resupply boxes and mailed general delivery to towns along the way or hand delivered by friends.

I’m still training hard with Shawn Austin over at Club Form. I never thought running stairs or walking up steep trails with a sandbag would be fun — and I still don’t — but Shawn makes it kind of fun. Her workouts have made me strong. A week ago I ran up Georgia Pass to check out the snow conditions — the pass is my fitness yardstick — and did a personal best, three hours and 50 minutes. OK. I know people who can do it in 2½ hours. They’re all younger than me.

The exact route I will take on The Colorado Trail is still up for debate. Most hikers start from Denver at Waterton Canyon and go south. I’m thinking of starting from Durango and walking north. Northbounders have some special challenges. The trail climbs fast onto the spine of the San Juan Wilderness and stays high and wild for 150 miles. North is a lonesome route. There are few fellow travelers. But on the plus side, going north, you meet nearly everyone coming south. Conversation with another human aside from yourself can be helpful at times.

These San Juan passages are the furthest from resupply of any part of The Colorado Trail. From Durango it is 72 miles to Little Molas Lake, and from Little Molas Lake to U.S. 50 it’s 150 miles. There are places I can mail packages to: the store at Little Molas Lake, post offices in Silverton, Lake City, Creede, Salida and Gunnison. Friends may meet me. We’ll see. Arranging meetings in weird wild places with sketchy connectivity can be challenging.

I feel ready. There’s still time to figure the route and logistics out. But not much time. That’s the thing with an adventure. I can plan and prepare and think about things, but in the end what will happen is I’ll pick up the pack and begin the walk.
