The Hike

2. Walking Blind

Denver Post photo editor Dean Krakel plans to spend six weeks hiking The Colorado Trail. Trevor Thomas, who is blind, is undoubtedly the most fearless hiker this season.

Blind hiker Trevor Thomas blazes the trail

By Dean Krakel

Trevor Thomas, trail name Zero Zero, started hiking The Colorado Trail in June with his guide dog Tennille. His trail name refers to his vision — Thomas is blind.

Ever since beginning this column on preparing to hike The Colorado Trail, I’ve heard from all kinds people who are also getting ready to hit the trail. They want to know if they’ll be OK hiking alone, are there bears and mountain lions, will they be able to get through the snowfields, is the trail well marked, is it easy to get lost, is it dangerous?

I know those fears because I’ve had them, too. But after meeting Trevor Thomas, not a single one of them mattered anymore. Thomas is undoubtedly the most fearless hiker walking The Colorado Trail this season.

Thomas’s trail name is Zero Zero. It represents what he sees. Thomas is blind.

What! A solo blind hiker on The Colorado Trail? That’s crazy! The trail is challenging enough. I can’t walk a mile without bumping or brushing something, without stumbling over stones, rocks and roots. What about spotting that gnarly cow moose up ahead, that slippery stream crossing, those storm clouds boiling on the horizon? Plus, I’ve misplaced the trail more times than I care to admit.

Blind hiker Trevor Thomas takes a selfie with his dog, Tennille, on The Colorado Trail. Photo by Trevor Thomas

So just how does a blind hiker navigate 486 miles of obstacles from Denver to Durango?

“There is no margin for error,” Thomas said by satellite phone from the summit of Georgia Pass. He was sitting in a snowbank, “Loving life but getting eaten alive by mosquitoes.”

“I use every resource available and all my senses to navigate,” Thomas said. He said uses echolocation, “like a human bat,” to know where he is and where he must go. “Everything in the world has a particular sound, and from those sounds I can get a primitive idea of what an environment is like.”

“Sound is Mr. T’s language,” says Thomas’ friend Laine Walter. “Footsteps are his music. He flies through the mountains.”

Walter is Thomas’ cheerleader, support crew and sometimes hiking partner. When Thomas decides to hike a trail, it’s Walter who condenses trail data, guidebooks and maps into a form he can listen to on his phone. Her audio guides are extremely precise; it sometimes takes her an hour a mile to create them.

She also organizes his resupplies, updates his Facebook page and talks to him daily on the satellite phone.

Trevor Thomas sets up his tent along The Colorado Trail. Most fellow hikers don’t even realize Thomas is blind. Photo courtesy of Dave Baumgartner

Thomas was blinded by an autoimmune disorder seven years ago, when he was 39. In eight months he went from thinking he needed glasses to fix some blurry vision to seeing everything in shades of gray and black. He’d been active all his life and had left a corporate sales job to attend law school. Going blind, he said, threw him into a “living hell.”

When Erik Weihenmayer — the Colorado athlete and adventurer who lost his vision as a teen and has since climbed Mount Everest and the rest of the Seven Summits and kayaked the Grand Canyon — visited Thomas’s hometown of Charlotte, N.C., Thomas was reluctant to attend the presentation. “The last thing I wanted to do was listen to another blind guy talk about how hard things are,” Thomas said. Instead, Weihenmayer inspired Thomas. The two became friends. “Erik gave me permission to be foolish.”

And the most foolish thing Thomas could think of was to thru-hike the Appalachian Trail. He became the first blind, solo, unsupported hiker to accomplish that feat. After that, he took on the Pacific Crest Trail.

Thomas calls The Colorado Trail one of the most challenging trails he has done, because of the altitude and the unsettled, often violent weather — “I can get struck by lightning just like any other hiker,” he said. “I don’t have any storm super-sense.” And then there’s the lingering snow. “Snow is a blind person’s navigational nightmare. You can’t feel the trail.”

The Colorado Trail is more remote than the Pacific Crest and Appalachian Trails. There are fewer fellow travelers, towns are further apart. But he does have one constant companion, who he credits for much of his long-distance hiking success — a black Labrador retriever named Tennille. Tennille guides Thomas around boulders, finds trail markers and keeps Thomas true to the trail. She can find water ... and even sniff out Starbucks and Coca Cola in the stores. “She points out the things that can hurt me, “ Thomas said. “She knows how tall I am and warns me about low hanging tree limbs. No other dog has been trained to do this before. She’s one of a kind.”

Thomas began hiking The Colorado Trail on June 19 — early enough to encounter a difficult mashed-potato spring snow ascent over the top of the Tenmile Range between Breckenridge and Copper Mountain.

“When you hike a long distance trail everything in your everyday life gets stripped away, reduced to the basics,” Thomas said. “You find out about yourself.

“On the trail there’s a common thread among hikers, from blacksmith to rocket scientist: We’re all out there looking for something.”

And what is Zero Zero looking for?

“I’ll just keep hiking and hiking and let you know when I find it.”

Trevor Thomas stands on an overlook along The Colorado Trail with his dog, Tennille. Photo by Dave Baumgartner

How Trevor Thomas is giving back

“When you’re blind,” Thomas said, “all you hear are people telling you what you can’t do. Blind kids get an especially raw deal in life. They’re set up to fail. I want to show them what’s possible. I want to share with them the confidence hiking has given me in other areas of my life. I want to give back.”

Thomas started the Team Farsight Foundation in 2009 to “challenge societal misconceptions about the abilities of the blind and visually impaired and to provide inspiration, hope, and assistance to other blind individuals who want to achieve goals not typically expected from members of the blind community.” To find out more about Team Farsight: blindhikertrevorthomas.com

Thomas is hiking The Colorado Trail to raise money for Guide Dogs for the Blind. Guide-dog-puppy raisers are helping Thomas resupply along The Colorado Trail: guidedogs.com

Thomas is also partnered with The Perkins School for the Blind and Boy Scout Troop No. 3 in Manchester, Mass., to develop the Blind Ambassador Program, a unique hiking program for people who are blind and visually impaired: www.perkins.org
