Walking long trails alone is a head game

By Dean Krakel

This is the question I get most often about The Colorado Trail: “Do you think I’ll be okay doing it solo?”

So there I was, standing at a trailhead along U.S. 50 west of Salida last summer, all ready-set-go for a Colorado Trail adventure. I’d taken three weeks off of work to do this. The plan was to hike a 160-mile loop of the Collegiate East and the brand new Collegiate West trail. After that? I’d just see where I ended up. A simple enough plan.

A bold enough plan that I was experiencing some doubt.

That’s mighty big, wild country out there pardner, I was thinking. A lot to take on alone. So I did what any other bold, adventurous hiker would do — I drove to Salida and had another cup of coffee.

I’m no stranger to doing things alone. I’ve skied for a month through Yellowstone National Park, solo biked Utah’s White Rim and kayaked the Green River through Labyrinth Canyon alone. From past experience I know that at the beginning of any adventure, there will be a moment before committing when the “I don’t think this is such a hot idea” voice chimes in.

The mental game is huge when you’re in the woods. Alone, the ante is upped considerably. I’m more alert. More cautious. More in tune with what’s going on around me and inside of me.

These are all good things, but there’s a downside: There’s no one to commiserate with about aches and pains, no one to consult the map with, or share a difficult passage, or speculate about the weather. No one to laugh or joke or share the beauty and joy with. Yet the majority of the Colorado Trail hikers I met last summer were solo. Many were women.

And that’s the question I get most often about the trail: Do you think I’ll be OK doing it alone?

It’s the first rule of the woods — never go alone. But people do it, and there’s even beauty in it.

Elizabeth Thomas stands on Sentinel Mountain in Olympic National Park. She often hikes alone. Courtesy of Grant Sible

“Hiking solo can be more rewarding,” said Elizabeth Thomas, a Trail Information Specialist with the Continental Divide Trail Coalition in Golden. “You get to make your own decisions. You can go where you want when you want. Eat what you want. Get up when you want. Make camp when you want. And you’re far better off hiking alone than hiking with someone you’re incompatible with.”

Thomas holds the women’s unsupported speed record on the Appalachian Trail — 2,186 miles in 80.5 days. She has walked over 13,000 miles on the long trails, many of them alone.

“I never cease to be amazed how empowering hiking is,” she said. “How exhilarating it is to do the seemingly impossible on nothing but my own two feet.”

But even if you’re healthy and strong, walking long trails is still a head game.

“When it comes right down to it, hiking the long trails is more of a mental game than a physical game,” she said. “Not quitting can at some points be challenging.”

Last July, when I was back at that coffee shop in Salida, I finished my coffee and drove back to the trailhead. I would do things one step at a time, I decided, one day at a time. I gave myself permission to quit or turn back at any time but not now, not at the beginning.

I loaded the pack, put it on, adjusted it and took the first step.

Elizabeth Thomas on The Colorado Trail. Thomas has walked thousands of miles solo and calls hiking long trails “more of a mental game than a physical game.” Courtesy of Brian Davidson

Hiking in good headspace

What are the keys to a good mental game in long distance hiking? Elizabeth Thomas shared this list of important points for those hiking in a group or solo. For more about Thomas go to: www.eathomas.com

1. The first thing you have to do is be honest with yourself about why you’re hiking a trail, she says. “That’s what’s going to get you through the hard moments.”

2. Write down why you want to hike a long trail, then announce that you’re doing it to your friends and family so you can call them up when you’re having a hard time.

3. Map out all the things that can go wrong and think about what you would do in that situation. Take a wilderness first aid course. Knowing what to do makes everything less of a crisis.

4. Being lost is taboo in our culture, but it’s probably going to happen on the trail. Try to be OK with it.

5. Pay attention to the many little mental games: eating before you’re hungry, putting on a jacket before you’re cold, making camp before your knee swells as big as a cantaloupe.

6. Nutrition is a big deal mentally. Don’t set up camp and go to sleep without eating — you need food to rebuild your body. On days in town, if you’re hungry, eat as much as you want. Soon you’ll be itching to get back on the trail.

7. Break your trip into bite-sized portions. Don’t think: I’m walking all the way to Durango. Think: I’m walking to Twin Lakes, or I’m walking to Mt. Princeton Hot Springs, or think about things that keep you excited for the trail.

8. The main mental issues for women are safety concerns. “When I first start out, that’s always in the back of my mind for awhile and then night after night it slowly fades away,” Thomas said. “Nothing bad is going to happen. You shouldn’t camp near roads — always try and be a mile or two away from a road. Most bad things come in cars.” Also, hitching into towns is probably the most dangerous thing women do on long hikes. “You have to be really smart about how you hitch,” she said. “Find somebody to hitch into town with.”
