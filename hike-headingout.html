3. Heading Out

Denver Post photo editor Dean Krakel plans to spend six weeks hiking The Colorado Trail. As he heads out, he’ll carry with him trail wisdom learned form readers’ experiances on the trail.

Packing reader advice and wisdom for the long walk

By Dean Krakel

G

od willing, and assuming the truck didn’t break down on the way to Durango, I’m walking The Colorado Trail this morning (July 28, 2015).

There’s great joy in finally beginning the long walk, all 566 miles of it (now that I’ve decided to include the Collegiate West trail). Thankfully preparation for the trail is mostly over — all the planning, training, food dehydrating, gear sorting, gear weighing, gear buying and packing and unpacking that gear again and again that has consumed the past 10 months.

When I began this Colorado Trail journey I knew that I’d have to do it alone. I’m good with that. Some adventures are solo ones, and that’s just the nature of the beast.

The thought also crossed my mind that doing the whole trail might somehow involve unemployment. Even in the beginning, the plan seemed too big and bold to fit into my work-a-day life. So I am leaving my staff position here at the Denver Post and embarking on a different path in life, walking a long trail into an unknown future. (But you’ll still be able to follow my hike at TK.)

Am I scared? Hell yeah. But at age 62 I am more afraid of leaving things I want to do undone in life than I am of unemployment. It’s all part of a lesson I learned in the three weeks I spent on The Colorado Trail last year.

Twin Lakes as seen from The Colorado Trail near Twin Lakes, Colorado last year. At Twin Lakes the Colorado Trail divides. One portion of the trail travels southwest along the lake shore and becomes The Collegiate West trail which travels for 80 miles south and is mostly above tree line. The other portion travels southeast for 80 miles. Together the two form the Collegiate West loop trail. The Collegiate West trail was just completed and opened in 2014. Dean Krakel, The Denver Post Photo by Dean Krakel

In my first Long Haul column this spring, I wrote about a frightening few minutes when I realized my heart was skipping some beats … well actually a lot of beats. We all know that there are inherent risks to walking through the woods alone. A lot can happen. Storms. Lightning. Falling rocks. Dead trees crashing down. Fire. Flood. Hypothermia. Heatstroke. Heart attack. It’s one thing to think about those risks in the comfort of home and quite another to be out there in the wild mountains getting smacked alongside the head with mortality.

After my heart settled on last year’s hike, I found a quiet inner peace. There are worse ways to check out of this life then face down on The Colorado Trail. I realized then that in order to really embrace walking the trail I was going to have to relax and let go. Of everything.

One of the things that has helped me begin my walk this year is learning from readers’ experiences on The Colorado Trail. As I head out, I’ll be thinking about some of the trail wisdom they’ve shared … and here’s a sampling:

“Stargazer”: (I) learned a thing or two on the trail that I consider invaluable. I leaned a lot about who I am, what I truly believe about people in general, and what I now know I am strong enough and capable enough to accomplish.

“Snorkel”: The process of walking a long trail is incredibly healing, and reminds us that we — not our bank or our boss — can control of our lives and how we spend our days. Hiking reminds us what is important, restores our faith in humanity as we meet kind strangers along the way, and reconnects us to ourselves, our communities, and nature.

Donna: I started the trail in late August and planned to be in Durango in early October. I’m 67 and my pack weighed 40 pounds. Of course, I read everything I could about the trail, had good equipment, was in what I considered good shape, mentally and physically. I averaged 8 miles a day. The blogs that show hikers averaging 15 miles a day are unbelievable to me.

I met the greatest people, saw unbelievable scenery, heard the elk bugle and loved it. I especially loved the part where you take your pack and shoes off for the night. I was very disappointed in myself for not completing the trail, but I’ll be back.

“Red Dog:” For several years I didn’t carry a stove, it was tuna in foil packets and tortillas. Then I went for the dehydrated food in water that I set up in a container in the morning and carried. My container was a Nalgene bottle I painted black and I put the it on top of my pack to be heated by the sun as I walked.

“Mr. Oddity”: The CW (Collegiate West) took me six days. The weather was poor and I lost a lot of time hiding from the thunder god. The long stretches of exposure are what make it a challenge, you are always watching for storms. … I had two of the scariest experiences of my life on that stretch. But my memories are all positive.

Gary: When I first saw your plan to do mega mileage every day, I thought “he’s going to have to move in the monsoon weather”! Lighting has been a hobby since my friend Mike was killed in 1971. There is no real science to lightning. … If you’re not packing golf clubs the odds are in your favor. I think the numbers are very small that you would get hit. I try and remember that every time I’m in a storm. Not very reassuring.

Kendall: My 75-year old mother has dreamed about doing something like this for a long time and it is now becoming a reality for her. She has worked so incredibly hard to start this journey … with my 19-year-old son. My mom has told me that if she dies on this trip, she has died doing what she wanted and loved the opportunity to do it. My son, on the other hand, has said she had better not die!
