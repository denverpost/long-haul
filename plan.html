The Plan

Denver Post photo editor Dean Krakel plans to spend six weeks hiking the Colorado Trail — 489 miles from Denver to Durango that cross eight mountain ranges.

It takes a lot of preparation to face the Colorado Trail

By Dean Krakel, The Denver Post

There was a moment on the Colorado Trail last summer when I thought I was going to die.

As I climbed a small, timbered ridge, my heart started missing some beats. A lot of beats. Thump thump thump … long pause … thump thump thump. I had a minor freak-out for a few minutes, right there in the woods: Should I hike to the next trailhead, hitch out and find a clinic? Should I end the trip, go home and make an appointment with a cardiologist?

I was one week into a three-week solo trip. It’d rained every day. Blood and smashed mosquitos smeared my legs. I’d been sandblasted by a dust devil, pounded by hail and had a come-to-Jesus moment with lightning. In that moment, I didn’t think nature was going to do me in. My heart was.

AA backpacker on the summit of Lake Ann Pass outside Buena Vista on the Colorado Trail. Photo by Dean Krakel

The Colorado Trail had proven more difficult then I had anticipated. Despite all my previous experience in rambling around the woods in 62 years, the trail’s learning curve was steep. I’d always backpacked with a heavy load, plodding along in big boots. Traveling across high terrain with running shoes and a light pack was something new and different. Alas, I made mistakes and traveled neither fast enough nor light enough. I didn’t fuel myself properly, and my conditioning was lacking. The mental game — being alone, constantly being chased off high places by storms, wondering what the point of all this effort was — was huge.

My heart steadied itself again that day on the trail last summer, and all winter I’ve planned my return. This time I want to walk the entire trail, from Denver to Durango — 489 miles. And instead of last year’s more casual approach, I’ve grown serious about conditioning, nutrition and gear.

The Colorado Trail

Stretching almost 500 miles from Denver to Durango, the trail has an average elevation over 10,000 feet and it rises and falls dramatically. Users traveling from Denver to Durango will climb a total of 89,354 feet.

Now, I’m all about miles, ounces and pounds, distances from here to there, the weight of gear and food, the nutritional density of meals. I keep a digital cooking scale on my home desk. The dehydrator runs ‘round the clock. A small mound of equipment changes shape daily as I change my mind, add and subtract. My maps and notebooks are covered with scribbles, notes, numbers and dates, distances between resupply points, the days it will take to get from here to there and how many calories I’ll need to fuel the journey. It’s like living inside a standardized-test question: “If Dean walks from point A to point B, carrying X number of pounds, how long will it take him and how many calories will he need? And, in Buena Vista, how quickly can he get his hands on a double cheeseburger, fries and a chocolate shake at K’s Dairy Delite?”

I’ve been working out, hard, with a personal trainer at the gym. I’ve run the local trails all winter. A few weeks ago, I went to the doctor. If something in your body is scary, I believe the best thing to do is procrastinate. But my heart is sound, my numbers were all good. If my resting heart rate and blood pressure were any lower I’d be a lizard. The doc believes the heart thing was a matter of hydration, altitude, exertion and low energy.

A Magical Place

I’m not worried about my heart this year — I’m worried about just getting to the trailhead after all this planning. Because despite the difficulty, the Colorado Trail is a magical place, filled with beauty and challenge. In both the best and worst moments, all the excesses and complexities of my everyday world are stripped away.

I know the truth is out there. I just have to walk far enough to find it, and 489 miles is a good start.
