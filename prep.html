The Prep

Food

Denver Post photo editor Dean Krakel plans to spend a month this summer trekking the Colorado Trail. He’ll need a ton of calories even as he packs light.

Packing in calories for the big hike with pemmican

By Dean Krakel, The Denver Post

Food? That stuff is heavy!

I’m a trail runner, and trail runners can run long distances on nothing but energy drinks, gels, shot blocks and bars, right? A guy walking a mere 15 miles a day on The Colorado Trail should be able to do the same!

In a fit of bad judgment last summer, I pulled the snack food, extra energy bars and heavier meals out of my pack to lighten the load at the beginning of a 75-mile-segment of trail, from U.S. 50 to Twin Lakes. By making my pack lighter I expected to be able to move faster. I figured I could get to my resupply load at Twin Lakes in three days.

Reading up on trail nutrition

“Pack light. Eat right” by Brenda L. Braaten for more on nutrition for long-distance hikers, as well as this interview with sports dietitian Tavis Piattoly.

Calorie-burn calculators

A quick round-up of a few online estimates (and guesstimates), plus some useful tools, for gauging your total caloric burn on your next backpacking adventure.

It was an enlightened take on lightening my pack: Without the encumbrance of excess food, the great outdoors would become a form of nutrition, surely. Those amazing vistas. That supercharged high altitude air. And losing some body fat might actually help energy to flow, the chi to arise.

Delusional.

Oh sure, the trail energy flowed, until day five — and I still wasn’t to Twin Lakes yet. I was heading north from Clear Creek, cruising an easy stretch of single track on a chilly, rainy day, bound for my resupply, just 6 miles away. I thought I’d been eating enough, but my caloric gas tank simply tapped out. I bonked so hard I couldn’t take another step and had sit down beside the trail. I needed food, but due to my pack-lightening experiment, most of the food had mostly run out. The next six miles had to be the longest and slowest six miles in the history of The Colorado Trail. I bonked twice more before I reached Twin Lakes. By then, the thought of a hamburger and a cold beer had nearly driven me mad.

Trail nutrition is serious business. A backpacker on the Colorado Trail hikes a half marathon, or more, day after day for over a month, burning upward of 6,000 calories a day. The balance between calorie count, pack weight and distance to resupply is a calculation that has to be tallied correctly. Last summer I was barely meeting my baseline calorie needs, much less eating enough to carry a 27-pound pack all day every day.

As I plan my return this July, my favorite buzzwords are “nutritionally dense.” I’m experimenting with making pemmican, a light-weight, energy-packed American Indian food adopted by the frontiersmen of yore. In yonder days pemmican was made with dried buffalo meat crushed into a powder by rocks and blended with buffalo fat. The resulting mixture provides energy from fat and strength from protein. Stored in a cool, dry place, pemmican lasts forever. The Lewis and Clark expedition ate a ton of pemmican during their two year journey from St. Louis to the Pacific Ocean and back. Polar explorers Vihljamur Stefansson and Robert Perry (and their sled dogs) subsisted on pemmican, and little else, for months at a time.

Dean Krakel, The Denver PostPemmican cakes are powdered lean beef mixed with fat and blueberries, cranberries and cherries. One cake has between 700-800 calories and weighs 4 ounces.

To make pemmican I cut lean grass-fed beef steaks into thin strips, then slow dry them in the dehydrator. The meat is powdered in a blender and mixed with melted tallow and dried cherries, cranberries and blueberries. I put the mix in muffin tins to harden. One of my 4-ounce pemmican cakes contains between 700-800 calories.

Of course, pemmican’s an acquired taste, not for everyone. My first batch tasted like beef-flavored cardboard. Yummm. I’ve since added berries, and it’s much more flavorful now. But pemmican isn’t about taste, anyway — it’s about fueling the machine.

In addition to making pemmican, I’m dehydrating chili, various crock-pot concoctions, eggs (a dozen dehydrated eggs weighs only 5 ounces), pasta, kale, refried beans, apples, sweet potatoes, red peppers and more. I’ve vacuum sealed freeze-dried servings of macaroni, stroganoff and lasagna, and stocked up on muesli, hard cheeses, tortillas, almond butter, dried fruit, berries, nuts and … Snickers bars.

Yeah, food is heavy. Ray Jardine, the father of ultra light backpacking compared food to rocket fuel. Rocket fuel is heavy too, said Mr. Jardine, but it gets the space ship into orbit.

Training

Denver Post photo editor Dean Krakel plans to spend a month this summer trekking the Colorado Trail. He turned to trainer Shawn Austin to get tuned up for the trail.

Getting trail strong with a drill boss

By Dean Krakel, The Denver Post

In the midst of doing yet another leg-quaking set of pulsing lunges combined with dumbbell bicep curls, one foot balanced on a Bosu ball, I ask myself, “Is this as hard as the trail?”

No. Not as hard as The Colorado Trail.

Last year I trained for the trail mainly by running. I ran over the top of Georgia Pass — 11,598 feet in elevation — three times. I swam and hit the gym. I felt pretty good. I figured after couple of weeks on the trail, I’d be a lean, mean hiking machine, bounding up the trail like a bighorn sheep. How hard can it be?

Turns out The Colorado Trail can be pretty hard. Harder than I expected. Walking long distances with a load on your back, huffing up passes, scrambling over scree, jumping streams, dodging lightning, hopping boulders, balancing on logs, swatting mosquitoes, packing and unpacking gear, walking in rain, snow, sleet, hail, wind and sunshine day after day, all by your lonesome — it takes its toll. During my attempt last year, a bounding bighorn I never became.

In preparing for this year’s adventure I wanted to train harder and smarter. The intensity is amped up a bit because I have a four-week window in which to walk nearly 500 miles of trail and climb 75,000 feet through eight mountain ranges and six wilderness areas. I want to be strong for that.

Since I train pretty much all the time, anyway, a Colorado Trail trip is a glorious opportunity to knock myself out completely. As a special treat I decided I wanted workouts so hard I wouldn’t do them unless somebody made me do them. Enter Shawn Austin, a personal trainer for Body by Emilee at Club Form. Shawn’s holistic, multifaceted approach to fitness — she is a nutritionist, yoga teacher, trainer and endurance athlete — is exactly what I needed to get tuned up for walking the trail.

Twice a week, every week since November, Shawn has been teaching me creative and painful things to do with dumbbells, benches, sliders, rings, cuffs, Bosu balls, kettle bells, cables, straps and anything else you can find in a gym (and some things you can’t). Push-ups, crunches, stair sprints and the much-despised Tabata intervals — a series of exercises done in explosive bursts, for 20 seconds on, 20 seconds off — Shawn’s workouts combine cardiovascular, core, leg, arm and shoulder strength with balance, coordination, ankle and foot stability. No two workouts are alike, though they’re all specifically aimed at making me trail strong, finishing each day feeling good, not face down in the pine needles.

Strength training for endurance hikers

Provided by Shawn Austin, ACE-certified personal trainer, Precision Nutrition Coach in training

KEY MUSCLE GROUPS WORKED — AND WHY

Abdominal muscles: Your core is responsible for stabilizing your pack when you twist and turn, powering you forward and up steep climbs and slowing you down to control descents.

Back and Shoulders: The muscles of the upper and mid back continually contract trying to stabilize the pack. Building up these key muscles can prevent backpackers from developing pain between the shoulder blades.

Strong shoulders can handle the angular motion of loading a pack onto one's back. This movement can put weak shoulders at risk for a rotator-cuff injury.

Legs: The weight of your pack and the impact of continuous hiking falls mostly on your two legs. Strong legs are key to both the success and enjoyment of your backpacking trip. Weak stabilizer muscles can lead to severe cramping and put your knees and ankles and risk for rolling — or worse.

The workout:

Perform the warm-up once. Do two sets of the strength training exercises, resting between sets.

Warm-up: 4 rounds stair sprints, rest 10 seconds after each.

Legs:

• Pistol squats: Stand in front of a bench, box or other low object and reach one leg out in front of you, toes flexed up. Now sit back onto the object as slowly as you can. Using the strength of your legs and core, slowly stand back up without putting your foot down. Repeat 12 times, then switch legs. (Challenge: Remove the object you are sitting on and perform a free-form pistol squat.)

• Weighted lateral lunges: Hold two 10- to 15-pound dumbbells in your hands; toes point forward. Take a large step out to the right, bending into the right knee and framing it with the two dumbbells. To avoid collapsing into your knee, focus on bending at the waist, engaging the glutes, and sitting back into the lunge. With control come back through center and then lunge to the left. Repeat 24 times total.

• Goblet squat: Stand with feet slightly wider than hip distance, toes angled out. Holding a 15- 30-pound kettlebell between your legs, squat down, tapping the kettle bell to the floor. With control, slowly stand back up. Repeat 12 times.

Back and shoulders:

• Pull-up/assisted pull-up: With a wide grip on the pull-up bar, engage the muscles in you core, back and shoulders as you lift yourself up bending your arms at a 90 degree angle. Use the pull-up assist machine at your gym if needed. Repeat 10-12 times.

• Plank row: Come into a plank on the floor, holding a 10- to 15-pound weight in each hand. Walk your feet out a little wider than hip distance apart. Hug your elbow in by your side as you pull one of the dumbbells back to chest height, then return it to the floor. Repeat this rowing motion 24 times, alternating sides.

Abdominals:

• Mountain climbers: Starting in plank pose, lift your right foot off of the floor and bring your knee forward toward your nose. Straighten that leg behind you and place your foot down. Switch sides. If you are new to this exercise perform these reps slowly, or begin to move more explosively, essentially running while in plank.

• Forearm plank to plank: Begin in forearm plank, forearms pressed down to the mat/floor. Lift your right arm off of the floor and place your right hand down. Using your right hand press up to a full plank, now lifting on to the left palm as well. Lower your right forearm back down to the mat followed by your left. Do 12 repetitions leading with your right, then switch sides to lead with your left for 12 more.

In between reps and sets I pepper Shawn with questions about nutrition. The link between function and fuel, I have learned, is a critical one. Shawn’s basic philosophy: Don’t eat things out of packages or wrappers. Cut the sugar and caffeine. Eat real, whole food, mainly vegetables, things that just have one name. Hydrate. It’s a nutritional philosophy I’ve been living while I train with her, but I’m also incorporating it into my trip.

I also train several mornings or evenings solo at the gym, and run and walk carrying a weighted pack on the trails near my home in Conifer. On Saturdays, I either go big in the gym with Shawn or go big on the trail with my running partner, Julie. Once a week I hike, snowshoe or run 10 miles or more.

If you’re thinking it all sounds like a lot of training, keep this in mind: Last summer, I met hikers of all ages — 14 to 80 — and abilities on the trail. I met super-hikers hitting 30 miles a day and novices struggling to make six. One 56-year-old woman had walked from Denver to Durango three times. There were section hikers, day hikers, weekenders, thru-hikers. And they all had big smiles on their faces. Maybe it’s because there’s truth to a saying I’ve heard out there on the trail: You hike your own hike.
